In an era of on-demand streaming, where any song is a search away, you might expect radio to fade out. Yet billions of people around the world still listen every week. Far from being obsolete, radio offers things that algorithmic playlists simply can't.

## The human touch

A great radio host does more than play songs — they tell you why a track matters, share context, react to the news and build a relationship with listeners over time. Stations like [KEXP](/stations/kexp) and [KCRW](/stations/kcrw) have built global followings precisely because real people are choosing the music with care and knowledge.

## Discovery beyond the algorithm

Streaming recommendations tend to give you more of what you already like. Radio does the opposite: it surprises you. A curated music station like [FIP](/stations/fip) might follow a jazz standard with an African groove and then a film score — connections no recommendation engine would make. That serendipity is how many of us find our favourite new music.

## Live, shared and immediate

Radio is live. When something happens — breaking news, a big match, a cultural moment — stations like [BBC Radio 4](/stations/bbc-radio-4) or [NPR](/stations/npr) are there in real time, and you're experiencing it alongside everyone else tuned in. That sense of a shared moment is something a private playlist can't reproduce.

## Companionship

Radio keeps you company. It fills a kitchen in the morning, a car on the commute, a workshop in the afternoon. There's a reason people describe their favourite station as a friend: a familiar voice in the background makes the day feel less alone.

## The best of both worlds

Internet radio brings radio's strengths into the streaming age. You get the curation, the live feel and the companionship — plus the ability to reach thousands of stations worldwide, save favourites and pick up the song titles as they play. Open the [player](/#player) and rediscover what radio does best.
