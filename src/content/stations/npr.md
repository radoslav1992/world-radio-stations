## History

National Public Radio was incorporated in 1970 and began broadcasting in 1971, growing out of the United States' move to fund non-commercial public broadcasting. Its first major program, the afternoon newsmagazine *All Things Considered*, premiered in May 1971 and remains on the air today. The morning counterpart, *Morning Edition*, launched in 1979.

Rather than operating as a single station, NPR is a membership organization: it produces and distributes programming to a large network of independently licensed public radio stations across the country. Those member stations, many of them university- or community-owned, carry NPR shows alongside their own local content.

## Format and programming

NPR is best known for long-form journalism, interviews and cultural coverage. Its flagship newsmagazines are complemented by programs such as *Fresh Air*, *Weekend Edition* and a wide range of podcasts that have become central to the organisation's reach in the digital era.

The tone is measured and explanatory, with an emphasis on context and reporting from a broad network of domestic and international correspondents. Music and arts coverage has long been part of the mix, though news and talk dominate the schedule.

## Reach

Because NPR distributes through member stations, its programs can be heard on hundreds of frequencies nationwide, with each station serving its local market. The organisation also reaches a large audience online through NPR.org, its apps and a substantial podcast catalogue.

## Significance

NPR is one of the most recognised names in American journalism and a cornerstone of the public radio system in the United States. Its production model — national programming carried by locally owned stations — has shaped how non-commercial radio operates across the country.
